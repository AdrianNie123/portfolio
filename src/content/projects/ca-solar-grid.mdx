<PullQuote>
When rooftop solar grows fast enough, it stops being just an environmental story and becomes an operational one. I used California's public installation records and hourly grid data to show what that transition looks like in practice: how solar suppresses midday electricity demand, forces the grid to ramp up faster each evening to compensate, and how that ramping pressure nearly doubled over eight years — from roughly 1,000 MW/hour in 2015 to 1,600 MW/hour by 2022.
</PullQuote>

<MethodList>

- Assembled a dataset linking two sources that don't naturally talk to each other: ZIP-code-level rooftop solar installation records from the California Energy Commission (who installed panels, where, and when) and hourly system-wide grid signals from the California grid operator (how much electricity was being generated and consumed, broken out by source). Joining these required cleaning and standardizing across different geographies and time formats — the core data engineering challenge of the project.
- Tracked how the daily load shape — the curve of electricity demand from midnight to midnight — changed across 2015, 2018, and 2022. As more solar came online, midday demand dropped (solar was covering it), but the window between 4pm and 9pm became increasingly steep as solar fell off and households came home. This is the "duck curve": the grid needs much more flexible, fast-responding power to cover that evening ramp than it did before solar scaled.
- Measured how quickly the grid needed to change its output hour-to-hour, and tracked that figure as a monthly average from 2015 to 2023. Applied a 12-month rolling average to distinguish the long-run structural trend from normal seasonal swings. The result shows a clear, sustained increase — the grid's operational stress grew in direct proportion to solar adoption.
- Used a spatial autocorrelation test (Moran's I) to check whether rooftop solar installations were clustering geographically or spreading randomly across California. The finding: adoption clustered — coastal and suburban areas led, inland areas followed — which matters because concentrated adoption in specific areas puts localized pressure on local distribution infrastructure, not just the statewide grid.

</MethodList>

<Caveats>
The analysis documents correlation, not causation — the data is consistent with solar driving these grid changes, but the grid operator's signals also include wind, battery storage, and demand response in ways that can't be fully separated with public data alone. The geographic clustering finding (Moran's I) tells us where adoption concentrated, but not whether those areas experienced worse local grid outcomes — that would require utility-level feeder data not publicly available. Policy transitions like the shift from NEM 2.0 to the Net Billing Tariff are noted as context for inflection points in the data, not as estimated treatment effects.
</Caveats>
