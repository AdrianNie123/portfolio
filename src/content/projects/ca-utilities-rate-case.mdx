<PullQuote>
A California General Rate Case determines how much a utility is authorized to collect from customers — a proceeding that can take two years and move billions of dollars. Using only public FERC Form 1 and EIA-861 data, I reconstructed the Phase 1 baseline analysis that CPUC staff conduct before engaging with confidential utility workpapers, identifying PG&E's GRC-recoverable O&M at $4,099 per customer — 46% above the $2,811 California IOU peer average, growing at 8× the rate of SCE and SDG&E on a post-bankruptcy baseline.
</PullQuote>

<MethodList>

- Built an 18 utility-year panel (3 utilities × 6 years) from FERC Form 1 financial schedules and EIA-861 customer and sales data. Categorized O&M into five FERC account buckets and isolated GRC-comparable O&M — Distribution + Customer Service + A&G×70% — per standard CPUC practice that excludes production (ERRA) and transmission (FERC-jurisdictional) costs from the GRC scope.
- Modeled revenue requirement using the standard regulatory formula: rate base × authorized return + depreciation + O&M + taxes. Calibrated WACC at 7.5%, depreciation at 3.5%, and tax rate at 27%, with each parameter editable to test sensitivity to commission-authorized values. Documented assumptions step-by-step so the model is reproducible from the underlying filings.
- Ran OLS cost-driver regression of O&M on rate base, customer count, MWh sales, and a post-bankruptcy dummy for PG&E. R² improved from 0.658 to 0.925 after excluding PG&E's 2019–2020 outlier years. Rate base emerged as the dominant driver — every $1B increase in rate base is associated with ~$0.33B in O&M — consistent with California utilities' capital-intensive wildfire mitigation and grid hardening programs.
- Projected residential bill impacts using EIA-861 revenue class shares as a cost allocation proxy. Average monthly bill computed from a 500 kWh/month residential baseline, 3% O&M escalation, and 4% rate base growth — the same forward-looking parameters a staff analyst would apply before reviewing utility-filed forecasts.

</MethodList>

<Caveats>
The model handles the financial side of a rate case cleanly, but it cannot estimate the income-distributional impact of the resulting rates — which is the question that actually matters for affordability proceedings. Bridging that gap requires LEAD Tool data and ZIP-code-level consumption not available in FERC or EIA filings. The 26–33% revenue gaps across utilities reflect total utility revenue requirement (including ERRA and FERC-jurisdictional costs) compared against total electric operating revenues; this is expected and methodologically consistent, not an error. Depreciation, WACC, and tax rate are approximated; actual authorized values vary by proceeding and a filed GRC uses utility-specific figures from a cost-of-capital decision.
</Caveats>
