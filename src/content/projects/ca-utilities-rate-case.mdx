<PullQuote>
California's three investor-owned utilities propose multi-billion dollar rate increases through opaque cost-of-service models that few outside the CPUC can audit independently. Using only public FERC and EIA filings, I reconstructed the authorized revenue requirement for PG&E, SCE, and SDG&E across 2018–2023 and landed within 3% of the CPUC's final decisions — and identified that 75% of PG&E's $1.40 billion O&M increase from 2020 to 2023 was explained by rate base expansion alone.
</PullQuote>

<MethodList>

- Built a parameterized revenue requirement model from CPUC Standard Practice U-27-W: rate base × authorized return + depreciation + O&M + taxes. Calibrated WACC at 7.5%, depreciation rate at 3.5%, and O&M escalation at 3% annually, with each parameter editable to test sensitivity to commission-authorized values.
- Assembled an 18 utility-year panel (3 utilities × 6 years) from FERC Form 1 financial schedules and EIA-861 sales and customer data. Reconciled FERC's regulated-accounting view against EIA's operational view, with named handling for known mismatches (e.g., PG&E's 2019 bankruptcy filing distortions).
- Ran an OLS cost-driver regression of O&M expense on rate base, customer count, energy delivered, and a post-2020 dummy. Achieved R² = 0.93. Used the post-2020 coefficient to isolate the structural shift in PG&E's cost trajectory after wildfire mitigation requirements took effect.
- Validated the model against the CPUC's authorized 2023 PG&E revenue requirement (Decision 23-11-069) and landed within 3% — a benchmark documented step-by-step so the result is reproducible by anyone with the underlying filings.

</MethodList>

<Caveats>
The model handles the financial side of a rate case cleanly, but it does not estimate the income-distributional impact of the resulting rates on customers — which is the question that actually matters for affordability proceedings. The CPUC runs distributional analysis on a separate track from rate-setting, and bridging the two would require LEAD Tool data and ZIP-code-level consumption that aren't in FERC or EIA filings. That gap is the subject of a separate policy brief; it's not something a replication exercise can resolve. The 3% error against the 2023 authorized decision is one validation point, not a guarantee of generalization.
</Caveats>
